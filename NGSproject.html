The Octo Monster

    Intro

    The Octo Monster project, our maiden venture into crafting a ground-based enemy, marked a significant milestone in my game development journey. This essay explores how this intriguing creature was conceived, developed, and refined.

    Creating an attacking monster for the first time was a challenge. We had to figure out where to begin, and that's when we dove into the realm of AI systems. Our objective was to breathe new life into the enemy AI, and it was no cakewalk. The ForceSystem (FS) added complexity to the mix, but after diligent exploration, we managed to decipher its inner workings.

    With a plan in place, my colleague, Jahvairo Monkau, and I collaborated effectively. We established a work plan that clearly outlined our responsibilities. I took charge of the coding while Jahvairo focused on the animator. What made our collaboration unique was our decision to switch roles every 1-2 hours, ensuring a balanced approach to the project.

    States

    The Octo Monster operates in four distinct states: We kicked off the project by creating the Pathfinding script. Thanks to our understanding of the ForceSystem, this part was relatively smooth sailing. We even included a GIF to show how it all worked.

    Idle and Detection: We progressed swiftly with the Idle and Detection states. However, things got tricky when we tackled Fuzzy and Attacking. That's when we decided to bring in our Lead Developer, Berend, for help. It turned out that calling Attack and Fuzzy simultaneously caused an animator bug, and you can see it in the upper GIF.

    Attacking and Fuzzy States: These two states were pivotal in creating the Octo Monster's dynamic behavior. The Attacking state was designed to bring the creature to life during combat, while Fuzzy added an element of unpredictability to its actions.

    The visual appeal of the Octo Monster was crafted by Alex Kentie, who used VFX shaders to make its legs stand out. These legs not only bent automatically but were also easy to customize, adding a dash of visual sophistication to the Octo.

    In conclusion, our journey with the Octo Monster taught us valuable lessons in game development, particularly the importance of teamwork and creative problem-solving in this exciting field.

Radial Stamina Bar

    Intro

    Unity's shader system is like a magic spell for graphics in games. It's a special code that tells the graphics card how to paint pixels on the screen. Shaders have two main parts: Vertex Shaders move objects in 3D space, and Fragment Shaders color those objects in 2D. Unity makes it easier by giving us a visual editor to create shaders without needing to be a math wizard. So, with Unity shaders, you can make your games look awesome!

    Man, diving into Unity shaders was no walk in the park, let me tell you. It's like this whole new language of graphics sorcery. I had to wrap my head around how these shaders, like, magically change the way things look on screen. It felt like preparing for a tough exam, with all those shader functions and syntax to memorize. But, you know what they say, the more you practice, the better you get, and now I can make things look really cool with shaders.

    The Radial Stamina bar serves as a substitute for the previous stamina system due to popular demand within our development team, inspired by the stamina bar in Zelda Breath of the Wild. This collaborative project was undertaken in partnership with Jahvario Monkau. Given that the stamina bar employs a shadergraph, it necessitated my acquisition of knowledge pertaining to shaders and their integration within the stamina bar implementation.

    Code

    Learning Unity shader coding was a real challenge, just like tackling a tough assignment. At first, I had to grasp the concept of how shaders manipulate graphics. It felt like deciphering a secret code. Then, I delved into the shader language and its functions, which was like deciphering complex instructions. There were moments of frustration when my shaders didn't work as intended, just like when you hit roadblocks with an assignment. But, with determination and lots of trial and error, I started to understand how to control colors, lighting, and textures. Finally, I realized that shaders were like crafting a unique artistic expression for each object in the game. It was a demanding journey, but in the end, it was incredibly rewarding to see how shaders transformed the visual aspect of the game.

AdvancedMeleeSystem

    Intro

    Creating a captivating combat system in the world of game development is no easy task. The birth of the Advanced Melee Weapon Combat System was a complex journey filled with challenges. This summary offers a glimpse into the obstacles I faced while building this intricate system, with a special focus on animation frames. My goal was to make the combat system feel like real swordfights. Animation frames were at the core of this vision. They acted as the timing for parry and block actions, like the beats in a song, making the combat feel authentic.

    In the next sections, we'll explore how I conceived and designed this system, and how animation frames played a crucial role in bringing it to life. We'll also touch on the challenges I encountered, as every big project comes with its hurdles. Throughout, remember that the magic of game development often lies in the balance between vision and execution, with animation frames being a key part of that equation.

    Code

    Parrying and blocking stood at the core of the system, each posing its unique coding challenges. It wasn't just about making them work; it was about coding them to align seamlessly with specific animation frames. Players had to grapple with these techniques. To make the combat system more engaging, I employed SphereCast within the code. This technique introduced a radius to parry and block actions, making the combat interactions dynamic and responsive.

    Controlling the player character through the PCombat script was a journey in itself. The code had to be orchestrated in such a way that parrying, blocking, and attacking could harmonize perfectly with the animation frames. The devil was indeed in the details.

    Animator

    In this intricate world of coding, the animator took center stage. Their job was to bring the combat system to life, ensuring that every action, from parrying to striking, seamlessly blended with the gameplay. They were the architects of realism, making the combat experience both engaging and immersive.

    Posture, Parry and Collision

    The posture damage system added a layer of complexity that was deeply tied to the world of animation frames. For posture damage to make sense, parrying and strikes had to be executed within specific frames. This was a coding challenge that required precision.

    Advanced collision detection was an integral part of the combat system. It allowed players to experience precise interactions between their sword and an enemy's weapon. However, it operated within the intricate framework of animation frames, making the coding journey all the more challenging.

    Conclusion

    In crafting the Advanced Melee Weapon Combat System, I uncovered the delicate balance between art and science within game development. It beckons players to master precise timing while challenging developers to unravel creative and coding complexities. Animation frames emerged as the pivotal enablers, shaping an immersive combat experience.
